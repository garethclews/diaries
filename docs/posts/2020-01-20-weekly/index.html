Said the King to the River

  • aloysius
  • Monday, Jan 20, 2020

2020’s content is finally coming through. Lots of fun and intereting things to bring my brain slowly out of its slumber.

Post of the week

Not something I found interesting so much as terrifying. The Defense and Security Accelerator has announced contracts worth Â£4M to use ‘artificial intelligence’ in warships. Given how terrible AI is at playing war video games are we really pushing it into machines which can define the fates of nations? Yes. Yes we are.

You should take this with a pinch of salt. I’m conflating the reality of the matter and I imagine the use is far more mundane. The thought of it is still very skynet though!

Blogs and commentary

MIT’s deep learning series starts our year with a good description of what the current state of the art in deep learning is and the hopes for 2020.

Towards Data Science have had a very, very busy start to the year.

  • Making use of time features, in which you may learn a little physics.
  • Deep learning in brain-computer interface which is as futuristic as your favourite sci-fi novella.
  • A description of Uber’s deep learning stack and how it is one of the best.
  • Finally, a step back from the edge to provide a tutorial for understanding neural networks.

Other posts from around the web:

  • Nature feature a description of how hardware can evolve to improve machine learning capabilities of modern computers. The paper is expensive to purchase so I haven’t read it.
  • Microsoft made me happy (shocking I know) by pledging to become ‘carbon negative’ by 2050.
  • I’ve previously posted blogs from Vicki Boykis so sticking with this tradition we have AI won’t save healthcare. She has become one of my favourite voices in the machine learning world.
  • Tristan Handy (of data science roundup fame) has written a blog about the changing landscape of analytics. The central dysfunction of data section, in particular, felt very familiar to me through a lot of conversations I’ve had across government and industry. Please use this full url as it refers via Tristan’s newsletter.
  • DeepMind found a deep learning technique also works in human brains.
  • El Reg has their first AI roundup of 2020. Including a link to a post on how machine learning is helping to drive bad decisions in Hollywood.
  • This one is worth a look if just for the gif at the top! Via boingboing, we have when machine learning cheats.
  • Following up on a previous post describing how Shakespeare’s Henry VIII was probably about half written by John Fletcher is a discourse on how ML can change the way we read Shakespeare.

Propaganda

  • Lenses are amazing. I’ve talked about them previously but this week, here’s your chance to dig into the library a little bit.
  • Cabal is my favourite build tool. Well, when I couple it with nix it is anyway. Go find out a little more about it and some of its features.
  • Way back before Halloween I posted a math3ma blog about adjunctions. Junstin Le now brings more practicality to this idea by considering adjunctions in the wild and in particular looking at foldl.
  • I’ve started an audit on the coursera Julia Scientific Programming course. I’ve found the first week more elementary than I need but I’m looking forward to the rest. I’m working through the videos without touching the notebooks and writing my own .jl files and its definitely proving worth it. Give it a go if you’re interested in learning some Julia, its free after all!
