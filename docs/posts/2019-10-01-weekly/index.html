I’m back from leave. These are the things I found interesting in my week off.

News roundup

  • Is it tragic when a computer thinks it has painted a Picasso?
  • Facebook are working hard at not only tracking everything digital about you, they’re also moving into the physical realm by using AI to improve the tracking of your hand movements for VR headsets

Blogs and research

  • Got noisy data? Google’s AI team think a bi-tempered logistic loss function is a good call for you!
  • I’m a huge fan of horror films, including all of Boris Karloff’s monsters. Turns out you can do some pretty neat things with hierarchical clustering as a paintbrush for drawing images
  • If you think that postcode area, gender and date of birth is a suitable level of anonymisation you may want to think again. This work is in part done at Imperial College London, who have set up a nifty live app where you can see just how unique you are within those three data points for you. FYI, Fishman can only be identified exactly from his three points of data 63% of the time. Guess this makes me a sardine?
  • What do we do when we collect too much data? Given the demand for all of the data, right now (although we rarely get what we want) are we building a rod for our own backs?
  • I’ve received a few recommendations for a presentation given by Susie Lu on visualisation and luckily for us there’s a blog post about exactly that talk
  • Want to learn some maths? Want it to not make your brain melt? Here’s a good introduction to adjunctions where you only want things to be ‘pretty much’ the same - parts two and three parts of this blog have also now been posted so you can go as deep as you want in your understanding of this useful mathematical concept

Books and papers

  • In a paper written by unknown authors there is an extension of an NLP context tree into the world of equations, allowing for applications of deep learning for symbolic mathematics
  • Applications of Bayesian ensembles in NLP tasks is yet more work toward a scientific understanding of language

Tools of the trade

  • I became aware that people around me hadn’t heard of or used Kalman filters so I scoured the internet for the cutest drawings to help others understand them - I really wish I had this resource when I was teaching the topic to undergraduates
  • A long time ago, because I’m pretty lame, I read SICP for ‘fun’ - since then it seems that the “wizard book” is becoming ever more relevant for people outside of computer science
  • Working in a purely functional language? Want an amazing way to reference pieces of data? Build your own lenses is a 20 step program to teach you about these abstract but awesome things, and you don’t even remotely need a PhD to understand it here!
  • I was reminded of a good lecture from Dan Licata in 2012 about domain specific datatypes, if the content eludes you then I think that’s ok because the absolute most important part of this document is the opening gambit

Origin of the title

A slight mistake on what WSfL meant… it should have been Windows Subsystem for Linux.
